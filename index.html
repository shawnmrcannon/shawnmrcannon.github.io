To code or not to code?

One may ask, why does a Philosophy major turned Ford Credit employee wish to become a coder? Well, a degree in philosophy has served me in many aspects of my life. The skills I gained from that education includes being able to have an interesting conversation with almost anyone and, more importantly, great critical thinking skills which can apply to any career path. For example, the skills needed to master the basic foundations of Symbolic Logic go hand in hand with coding. Lastly, there is a great deal of philosophical concepts found in cutting-edge technology. Abstract concepts such as morality and consciousness tend to keep cropping up in the realm of technology and software design. Already having knowledge and some understanding of these concepts will provide an advantage to any coder. However, my desire to code goes beyond an interesting connection to an ivory tower degree. I had always wanted to go into coding since I started teaching myself HTML as a child. Yet, when I went to a teacher to ask for advise on how to start working towards making websites, software, and shaping the future of the internet, I was told that there was no future in coding. Small and impressionable I gave up on my dream of becoming a software engineer. We now live in a world which demands talented and creative coders; I believe that my talents and passion will be well placed in this role. For me, there is no greater joy than taking a difficult puzzle and solving it.
